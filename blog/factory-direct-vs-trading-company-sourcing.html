Factory-Direct vs Trading-Company Sourcing from China: Which Is Right for B2B Buyers? (2026)

By Asaptic Labs 2026-06-14 8 min read

For most B2B buyers, the key question is not whether to source from China but through whom — and the answer changes the price you pay, who is accountable when something goes wrong, and whether any compliance or QA layer actually exists between you and the factory floor. The four main models are trading company or agent, Alibaba marketplace, local distributor, and principal-reseller; each makes a different allocation of price, liability, and working capital between the factory, the intermediary, and the buyer. The right choice depends on order volume, product complexity, regulatory exposure, and your internal procurement maturity.

Bottom line up front: If you need low prices and can absorb full factory risk yourself, factory-direct or marketplace sourcing can work. If you need compliance accountability, a named counterparty, and QA gates built into the process, a principal-reseller model — where the intermediary buys and resells in its own name — is structurally different from an agent who earns a commission and disclaims liability.

When factory-direct (or direct marketplace sourcing) wins

Factory-direct sourcing makes the most sense when the buyer has the internal capability to manage the relationship end-to-end: procurement staff with China experience, an in-house QA or inspection process, import and customs expertise, and familiarity with the regulatory requirements for their product category. In these cases, removing the intermediary margin is a legitimate efficiency gain.

Alibaba and similar marketplaces are effective for buyers sourcing commodity or low-complexity goods in relatively small quantities where the risk of a non-conforming batch is manageable — the buyer can absorb a bad order, negotiate a refund, or switch suppliers without serious downstream impact. The platform's search and comparison tools are genuinely useful for initial market mapping even when the actual purchase goes through a more structured channel.

  • Internal China sourcing team in place
  • Product is commodity, well-specified, and low regulatory risk
  • Order volume justifies direct factory relationship management
  • Buyer can conduct or contract independent inspection
  • Import and compliance handled in-house

When a distributor or agent is the better choice

Local distributors solve real problems: regional stock, fast replenishment, local-language technical support, local warranty service, and certifications already done for the buyer's market. For buyers who cannot receive an ocean freight shipment from China, do not have import infrastructure, or need guaranteed next-day delivery, a distributor's margin buys a real service layer, not just a price premium.

A sourcing agent can also be appropriate when the buyer already trusts the agent, has visibility into the factory, and is comfortable that the agent's quality of oversight matches the risk profile of the product. The structural limitation is that agents — by operating on commission rather than as principal — do not carry liability in the same way a buyer or a named counterparty does. That is a design feature of the agent model, not a defect; buyers should price it into their risk model.

  • Need for local stock, fast delivery, or local technical support
  • Local market certifications required and already done by the distributor
  • No import infrastructure or small, irregular order volume
  • Agent relationship is well-established with verified factory access and track record

How deposit-first protects the buyer

The deposit-first model — where a sourcing retainer is paid before any factory engagement or procurement — solves an incentive problem that is endemic to commission-based sourcing. When an agent or trading company earns only on transaction close, the financial pressure is to close deals, not to screen them. A retainer-funded process reverses this: the sourcing party is compensated for the quality of the qualification work itself, whether or not a factory passes the screen.

For buyers, the practical effect is that the QA and compliance work happens before money moves to a factory, not after a defective shipment has already arrived. It also means a sourcing engagement that surfaces a problem and recommends against a supplier is a success, not a failure — the incentive structure supports honest reporting. How to verify a supplier

Regardless of sourcing model, buyer due diligence reduces risk. A credible verification process covers at minimum: business registration and legal entity check, factory audit or third-party inspection report, production capacity and lead time documentation, relevant product certifications (CE, FCC, REACH, RoHS, ISO, or product-specific standards), export licence or compliance declaration where applicable, and references or transaction history. For regulated goods — medical devices, electrical safety, food contact, chemicals, or export-controlled technology — the bar is higher and specialist review is typically required.

Frequently asked questions

What is a principal-reseller sourcing model?

In a principal-reseller model, the intermediary buys goods in its own name, holds legal title, and resells to the buyer. This means the intermediary is the contractual counterparty, carries QA and compliance accountability, and takes credit and inventory risk — rather than acting as a pass-through broker. Asaptic operates on this model: it is your counterparty, not your agent or facilitator.

Why does a trading company or agent carry less accountability?

A trading company or sourcing agent earns a commission or thin margin for connecting buyer and supplier. Because they are not the legal buyer of the goods and are typically not the named party on export or compliance documents, their accountability for product quality, regulatory status, and delivery failures is structurally limited. Contracts for agents commonly include liability disclaimers that leave the buyer with direct exposure to the factory.

What does deposit-first mean and how does it protect the buyer?

Deposit-first means the buyer pays a sourcing retainer before work begins. This aligns incentives: the sourcing party is compensated for the screening, verification, and QA work — not just for closing a transaction. It also signals buyer seriousness, which improves factory access. The deposit is not a payment to the factory; it funds the qualification stage. Factory payments come later, tied to QA milestones.

Is buying directly on Alibaba safe for B2B procurement?

Alibaba is a marketplace that connects buyers and sellers; Alibaba is not the seller or the buyer's counterparty. Trade Assurance provides limited payment protection for defined dispute scenarios, but it does not guarantee product conformance, regulatory compliance, export documentation, or after-sales accountability. For commodity goods the risk is often manageable; for regulated, technical, or high-value products, buyers carry more counterparty and quality risk than the platform's interface suggests.

When is a local distributor the right choice over factory-direct?

A local distributor is typically the right choice when the buyer needs local inventory, fast replenishment, local-language technical support, regional certifications already done for their market, or local warranty service that a China factory cannot easily provide. The markup is real, but so is the service layer. For high-volume, spec-locked, repeatable orders where the buyer has import capability and procurement maturity, factory-direct or a principal-reseller model can recover that margin while retaining compliance structure.
