Short answer

Singapore is a leading urban BESS market, with major projects such as Jurong Island ESS demonstrating grid-scale deployment. Two mandatory regulatory gates must be cleared for all BESS projects in Singapore: (1) EMA (Energy Market Authority) grid-connection approval and ESS licensing under the Electricity Act — covering connection to SP Group's 230/400 V, 50 Hz network, operational licensing, and any market-participation rules under the National Electricity Market of Singapore (NEMS); and (2) SCDF (Singapore Civil Defence Force) fire-safety approval — SCDF has published specific ESS Technical Requirements that constitute a mandatory project gate before installation and commissioning. Beyond these gates, the practical compliance gaps for Chinese BESS exported to Singapore are: (a) IEC 62619 cell and module safety evidence as the internationally expected baseline, which Enterprise Singapore (SS) and project specifications reference; (b) IEC 62133 and IEC 62933-5-2 where referenced in project or EMA technical requirements; (c) SCDF ESS fire-safety design documentation aligned with SCDF's published technical requirements — Chinese GB-standard fire documentation is not a substitute; (d) UN 38.3 transport test documentation mandatory under international transport conventions; and (e) PCS configuration for Singapore's 230/400 V, 50 Hz grid. Chinese GB/T 36558-2023, GB/T 34120-2023, and NB/T 42090-2016 represent the manufacturing baseline but are not accepted as substitutes for IEC or SCDF evidence in project or authority review.

UN 38.3 Transport Safety Testing — Mandatory for Lithium Battery Imports to Singapore Chinese BESS cell and module manufacturers are required to comply with UN 38.3 for export shipments under international transport conventions. Chinese manufacturers typically hold UN 38.3 test reports and test summaries from CNAS-accredited testing laboratories such as UL, SGS, Bureau Veritas, TÜV, or CAICT. The UN 38.3 Test Summary (required since January 1, 2020) must cover the specific cell or battery type being shipped. Singapore is a party to international transport conventions and serves as a major global transshipment hub; this requirement applies universally to all lithium battery imports by air (Changi Airport), sea (Port of Singapore), or road — there is no Singapore-specific exemption.
