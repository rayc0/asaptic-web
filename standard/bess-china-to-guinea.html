Short answer

Guinea does not have a confirmed standalone mandatory BESS product safety regulation enforced at the national level. The primary regulatory body for standards conformity is IGNM (Institut Guinéen de Normalisation et de Métrologie), whose current mandatory scope for BESS has not been confirmed from publicly accessible official sources. The utility EDG (Electricité de Guinée) is the grid connection authority; EDG technical connection specifications for BESS are not publicly available and must be obtained directly. Guinea's grid operates at 220/380 V 50 Hz — nominally aligned with China's domestic grid — which reduces PCS voltage reconfiguration risk compared with markets using 240/415 V. However, the practical compliance gaps for Chinese BESS exported to Guinea are: (1) no publicly confirmed IGNM mandatory conformity certification route for BESS — project and donor specifications typically drive IEC 62619 cell and module safety evidence requirements; (2) IEC 62933 system-level technical requirements referenced in donor-financed (World Bank, AfDB, IFC) project specifications; (3) EDG grid-connection technical approval — requirements must be confirmed directly with EDG before equipment design is finalised; (4) fire-safety installation requirements — Guinea has no confirmed BESS-specific fire code; IEC 62619/62933-5-2 and international best practice govern in donor project contexts; (5) UN 38.3 transport documentation mandatory under international conventions for lithium battery shipments through Port of Conakry; (6) humidity and dust derating — Guinea's tropical coastal climate imposes thermal and environmental stresses not addressed in Chinese domestic testing. Chinese GB 44240-2024 and GB/T 36558-2023 represent the manufacturing baseline but are not accepted as substitutes for IEC evidence in donor-financed project or authority review.

UN 38.3 Transport Safety Testing — Mandatory for Lithium Battery Imports to Guinea via Port of Conakry Chinese BESS cell and module manufacturers are required to comply with UN 38.3 for export shipments under international transport conventions. Chinese manufacturers typically hold UN 38.3 test reports and test summaries from CNAS-accredited testing laboratories such as UL, SGS, Bureau Veritas, TÜV, or CAICT. The UN 38.3 Test Summary (required since January 1, 2020) must cover the specific cell or battery type being shipped. A Chinese-origin UN 38.3 test summary from an accredited laboratory is acceptable for Guinea-bound shipments via the Port of Conakry — the key considerations are ensuring the test summary covers the specific cell model, chemistry, capacity, and configuration of the BESS units being shipped, and that it is maintained current with any cell design changes. Since January 1, 2020, a UN 38.3 Test Summary is mandatory documentation that must accompany lithium battery shipments under international transport regulations (IATA DGR, IMDG Code, ADR). Guinea is a party to international transport conventions. BESS cells and modules exported from China to Guinea are transported primarily by sea through the Port of Conakry — the principal deep-water port on the West African coast handling the majority of Guinea's imports. Hazardous goods handling procedures at the Port of Conakry, including documentation requirements for lithium battery shipments under the IMDG Code, must be verified with the port authority and the appointed shipping or freight-forwarding agent before shipment.
