China-to-EU Perovskite PV Module Compliance Gap Matrix

Beta · data under verification — informational only, not a certification conclusion. Public-interest, source-linked comparison of common China perovskite and tandem PV module documentation against EU CE, IEC module qualification, safety, environmental, and lead-restriction expectations.

Short answer

IEC 61215 and IEC 61730 module qualification, plus the RoHS lead-restriction question for perovskite Pb content, are the key gaps beyond conventional silicon PV modules.

GB/T 6829 / GB/T 17626 series (EMC, based on IEC 61000 — voluntary domestic reference)		 Perovskite photovoltaic modules placed on the EU market must satisfy a layered compliance package. (1) CE marking via product-safety conformity assessment: the primary harmonised standard is EN IEC 61730-1/-2 (PV module safety qualification); performance characterisation follows EN IEC 61215-1/-1-1/-2 (terrestrial PV modules). LVD (2014/35/EU) and EMC Directive (2014/30/EU) apply where the module or its integrated electronics fall within those directives' scope; standalone PV modules without integrated active electronics may fall outside LVD/EMC scope but manufacturers should verify applicability. (2) RoHS Directive (2011/65/EU, recast): PV modules are currently not listed in Annex I as in-scope equipment categories, but lead-containing perovskite formulations (e.g. lead halide perovskites) could trigger scrutiny under evolving substance restrictions; manufacturers should monitor EU RoHS implementing measures. (4) Economic operator: Regulation (EU) 2019/1020 requires an identifiable responsible economic operator established in the EU (manufacturer, importer, or authorised representative) whose details appear on the product or accompanying documentation and who holds the technical file. The EU Declaration of Conformity (DoC) and technical documentation file must be prepared and retained for 10 years. PV Module Type-Approval — Performance and Extended-Stress Reliability (IEC 61215 + IEC TS 63209) GB/T 37409-2019 is the principal Chinese national standard for PV module type-approval testing, closely aligned with IEC 61215 in test sequence and pass/fail criteria. Chinese certification (CQC, CGC) against GB/T 37409 is required for domestic grid-connection approval under the National Energy Administration framework. However, GB/T 37409 does not yet incorporate the extended-stress test sequences of IEC TS 63209 that are specifically intended to expose accelerated degradation in novel chemistries such as perovskite. Chinese manufacturers typically test to IEC 61215 directly for export certification rather than relying solely on GB/T 37409.GB/T 37409-2019 (Terrestrial photovoltaic modules — Design qualification and type approval) EU market access for PV modules (including perovskite and perovskite-silicon tandem) depends on demonstrating conformity with IEC 61215-1/-2 (design qualification and type approval — electrical, mechanical, thermal performance) and IEC 61215-1-1 (crystalline silicon) or the applicable technology-specific part. Power and energy rating must follow IEC 61853-1/-2/-3/-4. For emerging chemistries such as perovskite, extended-stress reliability testing under IEC TS 63209-1 (module-level) and IEC TS 63209-2 (bill-of-materials level) is increasingly required by financiers, insurers, and certification bodies to evidence long-term degradation behaviour under heat, UV, and moisture. CE marking for PV modules is not mandated by a single PV-specific EU directive; modules must satisfy the Low Voltage Directive (2014/35/EU) and, where applicable, the CPR (305/2011/EU) for building-integrated applications. EU Solar Energy Strategy COM(2022) 221 [UNVERIFIED — policy, not yet adopted regulation]		 The critical gap for perovskite PV modules is long-term reliability evidence. IEC 61215 type-approval was designed around mature crystalline silicon and thin-film chemistries; its standard damp-heat (1000 h, 85 °C/85% RH), UV (15 kWh/m²), and thermal-cycling (200 cycles) sequences were not calibrated to expose the accelerated ion migration, moisture ingress, and phase degradation mechanisms that are characteristic of perovskite absorbers. IEC TS 63209 extends these stresses (e.g., 2000 h damp-heat, extended UV, combined UV+damp-heat) to better discriminate long-term field degradation, but it is a Technical Specification (not a full IEC Standard) and test laboratories differ in how they apply it. EU project financiers, grid operators, and independent engineers (IEs) routinely demand IEC TS 63209 data before bankability sign-off for perovskite modules; CN domestic certification does not yet require this. The result is that a perovskite module passing GB/T 37409 and even IEC 61215 may lack the field-lifetime evidence required by EU off-takers, insurers, and lenders. No CN standard currently addresses perovskite-specific degradation modes. Additionally, Ecodesign/Energy-label obligations for PV modules remain unconfirmed [UNVERIFIED] and must be monitored.Attention required. Perovskite PV modules must complete IEC 61215 type-approval (available via accredited CB test labs) and, critically for EU bankability, must generate IEC TS 63209 extended-stress test data addressing heat, UV, and moisture degradation modes specific to perovskite absorbers. Passing IEC 61215 alone is insufficient for EU project finance and insurance acceptance. GB/T 37409 certification covers domestic CN grid-connection but does not substitute for EU-facing IEC 61215 certification or IEC TS 63209 reliability evidence. WEEE Producer Registration — PV Modules (Directive 2012/19/EU) China has no export-side obligation equivalent to EU WEEE producer registration. Domestically, the 'Regulations on the Administration of the Recovery and Disposal of Waste Electrical and Electronic Products' (废弃电器电子产品回收处理管理条例, State Council Order No. 551, 2009, amended 2019) impose collection-fund contributions and disposal obligations on domestic sellers and manufacturers, but these apply to products sold within China and do not create any corresponding registration duty for goods exported to the EU. Chinese PV module manufacturers exporting to Europe are therefore entirely unregistered on the CN side with respect to EU WEEE obligations.废弃电器电子产品回收处理管理条例 (State Council Order No. 551, 2009, amended 2019) — domestic scope only Directive 2012/19/EU (WEEE Directive, recast) explicitly includes photovoltaic panels in its scope (Annex I, Category 4: Large Equipment; and Annex III, Category 11: Photovoltaic panels, introduced via the recast). Producers — defined to include importers placing PV panels on the EU market under their own brand or as the first importer — must register in each EU Member State where they sell, finance end-of-life collection and treatment, and mark products with the crossed-out wheeled bin symbol. Member States have transposed the directive into national law with varying registration procedures, fees, and reporting obligations (e.g., Germany: ElektroG / EAR register; France: eco-organization scheme; Netherlands: Wecycle). A non-EU manufacturer must appoint a local Authorised Representative in each Member State to fulfil these obligations. Perovskite modules are in scope identically to crystalline silicon panels; no chemistry-specific exemption exists.Directive 2012/19/EU (WEEE Directive, recast) — Annex I Category 4, Annex III Category 11
Article 17 (Authorised representatives for non-EU producers)
Annex IX (crossed-out wheeled bin marking)		 WEEE producer registration is a concrete per-country administrative prerequisite with no Chinese analogue. For perovskite modules there is an additional concern: lead (Pb) is present in many perovskite absorber formulations (e.g., methylammonium lead iodide, MAPbI3). The presence of a regulated hazardous substance (lead) in the module raises the urgency of proper WEEE registration and end-of-life management, since uncontrolled disposal could result in lead leaching — a risk that regulators, NGOs, and EU member-state environmental agencies may scrutinise more closely than for standard silicon modules. This does not alter the registration procedure but it strengthens the compliance and reputational case for robust WEEE compliance. Additionally, perovskite modules are not yet subject to a chemistry-specific WEEE collection target, but the standard PV panel obligations apply in full. Chinese manufacturers frequently lack this registration; the per-country fragmentation and cost (authorised representative fees, eco-contribution levies) are the most common barriers.Attention required. Per-member-state WEEE registration (or appointment of an authorised representative) is a mandatory administrative prerequisite with no Chinese equivalent. For lead-containing perovskite formulations, proper WEEE end-of-life compliance also carries environmental and reputational significance beyond administrative box-ticking. Chinese perovskite module exporters to the EU must budget for authorised representative appointments and eco-contribution levies in each target market before first sale. RoHS — Lead (Pb) Restriction for Perovskite PV Modules (EU Market Access Risk) GB/T 26572-2011 (recommended, current) sets the same six restricted substances including lead (Pb) at the same 0.1% w/w MCV. The new mandatory standard GB 26572-2025 (effective 2027-08-01) expands the list to ten substances but retains the same lead MCV. Critically, neither the current nor forthcoming CN standard includes an exemption mechanism comparable to RoHS II Annex III. In practice, lead-based perovskite modules would face the same substance limit under CN rules; however, as GB/T 26572-2011 is currently recommended (not mandatory), enforcement for domestic-market products is less stringent. SJ/T 11364 requires a China RoHS hazardous substance label on the product. No CN-side perovskite-specific exemption process has been identified.GB/T 26572-2011 (recommended, current — Pb MCV 0.1% w/w)
GB 26572-2025 (mandatory, effective 2027-08-01 — same Pb MCV, no exemption mechanism)
SJ/T 11364 (China RoHS hazardous substance marking label)		 Directive 2011/65/EU (RoHS II) restricts lead (Pb) to a maximum concentration value (MCV) of 0.1% w/w at the homogeneous material level in electrical and electronic equipment (EEE). PV panels and modules were brought into RoHS scope under Category 11 (Other EEE not covered by other categories) by Directive 2011/65/EU as amended. Most high-efficiency perovskite solar cells use lead-based perovskite absorbers (e.g., methylammonium lead iodide, CH3NH3PbI3) containing lead at concentrations that likely exceed the 0.1% MCV — making them potentially non-compliant unless a specific exemption applies. Annex III of RoHS II lists time-limited exemptions for specific uses where technically or scientifically impracticable to substitute; Annex IV covers Category 8/9 medical/monitoring equipment. EN IEC 63000:2018 (harmonised standard for RoHS technical documentation)		 This is the single largest perovskite-specific EU market-access risk. The EU RoHS Pb limit (0.1% w/w homogeneous material) is likely exceeded by lead-based perovskite absorber layers; compliance therefore depends entirely on whether a valid Annex III exemption exists or can be granted. [UNVERIFIED: No confirmed Annex III exemption for lead in perovskite PV absorbers has been identified as of the accessed date — this must be verified against the live EUR-Lex RoHS II consolidated text and the EC exemption review register.] The CN equivalent (GB/T 26572-2011) currently imposes the same MCV but is only recommended, not mandatory, reducing immediate CN-side enforcement risk for domestic market. However, exporters to the EU face the full mandatory EU RoHS regime with no flexibility on substance limits absent a granted exemption. Additionally, REACH SVHC candidate list includes lead compounds (e.g., lead sulfate, lead chromate); lead(II) acetate and other lead compounds may trigger Article 33 SVHC notification obligations independently of RoHS.[UNVERIFIED — exemption status must be confirmed before placing product on EU market] Lead-based perovskite PV modules face a probable RoHS non-compliance finding under EU Directive 2011/65/EU unless a valid Annex III exemption for lead in perovskite absorber layers is confirmed as in force. This is the single most critical EU regulatory risk specific to lead-perovskite technology. Manufacturers must: (1) verify current Annex III exemption status against the live EUR-Lex consolidated RoHS II text and EC exemption register; (2) if no exemption exists, assess whether a lead-free perovskite formulation is technically feasible; (3) independently verify whether any listed REACH SVHCs (lead compounds) in the module exceed the 0.1% w/w Article 33 threshold.
