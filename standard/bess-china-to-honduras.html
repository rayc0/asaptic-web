Short answer

Honduras does not yet have a confirmed standalone mandatory BESS product safety regulation equivalent to an IEC 62619-based certification regime. BESS projects in Honduras operate under CREE regulatory oversight, ENEE interconnection agreements, and project-specific international standards referenced in procurement or financing specifications. The most significant practical gap for Chinese BESS exported to Honduras is the grid frequency and voltage incompatibility: Honduras operates at 60 Hz / 120–240 V (single-phase) and 120/208 V or 277/480 V (three-phase) — fundamentally different from China's 50 Hz / 220–380 V grid. Chinese BESS power conversion systems (PCS) designed and validated for China's 50 Hz grid require firmware reconfiguration, protection-parameter resetting, and re-validation before they can be connected to Honduras's 60 Hz grid. Other key gaps are: (1) IEC 62619 cell and module safety evidence, which international project financiers and CREE-licensed engineering reviews expect as the baseline; (2) IEC 62933 system-level requirements where referenced in ENEE interconnection or project specifications; (3) fire-safety installation approval from Honduran Bomberos and local building authority, with NFPA 855 as the internationally dominant reference for stationary BESS; (4) UN 38.3 transport documentation, mandatory for all lithium battery shipments under international conventions via Puerto Cortés (Caribbean, main container port) or San Lorenzo (Pacific); and (5) SIEPAC regional grid interconnection context — Honduras is part of the Central American regional electricity market (MER) and regional stability requirements may apply. Chinese GB/T 36558-2023, GB/T 34120-2023, and NB/T 42090-2016 represent the China manufacturing baseline but are not accepted as substitutes for IEC evidence in project or authority review, and are all based on 50 Hz grid parameters incompatible with Honduras.
