Short answer

South Sudan does not currently have a confirmed standalone mandatory BESS product safety regulation. SSNBS (South Sudan National Bureau of Standards), established under the South Sudan Standards, Metrology, Conformity Assessment and Accreditation Authority Act, has nascent institutional capacity and has not published BESS-specific technical regulations accessible from official sources as of the dataset date. The South Sudan Electricity Corporation (SSEC) is the national utility responsible for electricity generation, transmission, and distribution; SSEC's published grid-connection technical specifications for BESS are not publicly confirmed. The practical reality is that almost all BESS deployments in South Sudan are donor-funded or humanitarian (e.g., UNDP, World Bank, bilateral aid), off-grid or mini-grid in character, and governed primarily by the project-owner or donor's own technical specifications — which typically reference IEC 62619, IEC 62933, and UN 38.3. The key gaps for Chinese BESS exported to South Sudan are: (1) IEC 62619 cell and module safety evidence, which is the expected baseline in any international project specification; (2) voltage mismatch — South Sudan uses 230/400 V 50 Hz whereas China's grid is 220/380 V; PCS must be re-parameterised; (3) extreme heat derating — ambient temperatures regularly exceed 40 °C in South Sudan and BESS thermal management must be validated for these conditions; (4) IEC 62933 system-level evidence where required by the project or donor specification; (5) NFPA 855-aligned fire-safety design where referenced in project specifications; and (6) UN 38.3 transport documentation mandatory under international transport law. Chinese GB/T 36276, GB 38031, GB/T 34131, and GB/T 36558 are not accepted as substitutes for IEC standards in project or donor review. Transit logistics are complex — South Sudan is landlocked and primary routes are via Mombasa (Kenya) or Port Sudan (Sudan).

UN 38.3 Transport Safety Testing and Landlocked Transit Logistics — Mandatory for Lithium Battery Shipments to South Sudan Chinese BESS cell and module manufacturers are required to comply with UN 38.3 for export shipments under international transport conventions. Chinese manufacturers typically hold UN 38.3 test reports and test summaries from CNAS-accredited testing laboratories such as UL, SGS, Bureau Veritas, TÜV, or CAICT. The UN 38.3 Test Summary (required since January 1, 2020) must cover the specific cell or battery type being shipped. A Chinese-origin UN 38.3 test summary from an accredited laboratory is acceptable for shipments transiting Kenya, Uganda, and South Sudan — the key gap relative to straightforward export routes is the complexity of multi-modal landlocked transit, the need for dangerous-goods compliance in each transit country, and the need to work with experienced freight forwarders who know the Mombasa–Juba or Port Sudan–Juba corridors.
