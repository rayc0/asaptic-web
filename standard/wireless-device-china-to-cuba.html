Short answer

The main Cuba compliance gaps for a China-made Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, or IoT device are: MINCOM mandatory homologación under Decree-Law 35 (2021) — all radio and telecom terminal equipment requires MINCOM type approval before import or commercial placement, with no independent recognition of CCC, CE, or SRRC approvals (though Chinese supplier technical documentation is typically accepted given strong bilateral ties and ETECSA's established procurement of Chinese equipment); state-entity importer requirement — private importers are largely unavailable and equipment must be procured through ETECSA, CIMEX, or other MINCOM-authorized state enterprises; NC (Normas Cubanas) EMC compliance (harmonised with IEC/CISPR, assessed within homologación); compatibility with Cuba's dual-voltage system (110 V and 220 V, both 60 Hz) and Type A/B plugs — a universal 100–240 V / 50–60 Hz input is strongly recommended; and cybersecurity compliance under Decree-Law 35 (2021) and Decree 360 (2019) assessed within the MINCOM homologación technical file. Spanish labelling is required. US dollar restrictions from the US embargo affect payment routes — CNY and euro channels are commonly used. Cuba is a BRI member and China is its top trading partner; ETECSA extensively uses Chinese equipment (Huawei, ZTE), providing an established G2G procurement pathway.
