Short answer

Denmark fully applies the EU Radio Equipment Directive (RED 2014/53/EU); CE marking under RED is the primary gate enforced by Energistyrelsen (Danish Energy Agency). Key gaps versus Chinese SRRC/CCC include: radio performance testing to EN 300 328 / EN 301 893, EMC to EN 301 489 series, electrical safety to EN IEC 62368-1:2020+A11 (230 V/50 Hz, plug type K — Danish 3-pin earthed), cybersecurity under RED Art. 3.3(d)–(f) via EN 18031 (mandatory from 1 August 2025), EU Authorised Representative, Danish-language labelling for consumer products, and WEEE registration in Denmark via DPA-system. CCC and FCC are not recognised. Greenland and the Faroe Islands are outside EU jurisdiction — EU RED does not apply there.

MIIT Network Access Licence (NAL) — mandatory for terminal equipment accessing public telecom networks		 Denmark is an EU member state and fully implements the Radio Equipment Directive (RED) 2014/53/EU. CE marking is mandatory for all radio equipment (including Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and cellular IoT devices) before placement on the Danish market. The conformity assessment route for most Wi-Fi/Bluetooth products is the self-declaration pathway (Module A — internal production control): the manufacturer applies harmonised standards EN 300 328 / EN 301 893 (radio), EN 301 489-1 + EN 301 489-17 (EMC), and EN IEC 62368-1:2020+A11 (safety), draws up an EU Declaration of Conformity (DoC), and affixes the CE marking. The national regulatory authority is Energistyrelsen (Danish Energy Agency), which administers the Danish Radio Frequency Plan and enforces RED compliance through market surveillance and product checks. Sikkerhedsstyrelsen (Danish Safety Technology Authority) oversees product safety under LVD/RED Art. 3.1(a) and REACH/RoHS. Denmark uses 230 V/50 Hz mains; the domestic plug standard is DS 60884-2-D1 (Type K — a unique Danish 3-pin earthed plug with a round earth pin offset below the two power pins); power adapters and chargers must support plug Type K or include a universal/Type K adapter for consumer use. CCC and FCC certifications are not recognised in Denmark as substitutes for CE marking.
