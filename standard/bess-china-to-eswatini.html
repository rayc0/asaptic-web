Short answer

Eswatini does not yet have a confirmed, publicly documented mandatory BESS product safety or grid-connection regulation specific to battery energy storage systems. The country is a landlocked SADC/SACU member that imports the majority of its electricity from South Africa (Eskom) and whose grid interconnection practice is understood to align with South African NRS standards — though this has not been independently confirmed for BESS-specific requirements. The practical gaps for Chinese BESS exported to Eswatini are: (1) IEC 62619 cell and module safety evidence as the internationally expected baseline — Chinese GB/T 36276 and GB 38031 are not accepted substitutes; (2) ESERA licensing and EEC grid-connection approval, likely referencing IEC 62933 and NRS-family standards, though no confirmed public specification exists; (3) SWASA conformity marking, whose scope for BESS products has not been confirmed; (4) NFPA 855-aligned fire-safety installation documentation where referenced by the project authority; and (5) UN 38.3 and IEC 62281 transport documentation mandatory under international transport conventions. The 230/400 V 50 Hz grid shares China's 50 Hz frequency but differs from China's 220/380 V nominal voltage; the modest difference may require PCS parameter review, and ESERA and EEC requirements must be verified directly with those authorities.

UN 38.3 and IEC 62281 Transport Safety — mandatory for lithium battery imports via all modes Chinese BESS and lithium battery manufacturers typically hold UN 38.3 test summaries issued by CNAS-accredited or ILAC-member laboratories (including UL, SGS, Bureau Veritas, TÜV, CAICT). Chinese-origin UN 38.3 test summaries from properly accredited laboratories are accepted for international transport into Eswatini via South African ports and road crossings — this is not a standards equivalence gap but a documentation currency and scope gap. The key risks are: (a) the test summary must exactly cover the specific cell model, chemistry, capacity, and format being shipped in this consignment; (b) the issuing laboratory must still hold current accreditation; (c) any cell design change after testing requires a new UN 38.3 assessment before the modified cell can be shipped; (d) module and pack assemblies may require separate UN 38.3 assessment in addition to cell-level testing; (e) state-of-charge limits and packaging requirements under IEC 62281 and IMDG must be met for the specific shipment configuration.UN 38.3 test summaries from CNAS-accredited or ILAC-member laboratories

GB/T 28539-2012 (Safety requirements for transportation of lithium primary battery — Chinese domestic reference only)		 Eswatini is a landlocked country in Southern Africa. All BESS imports must transit through neighbouring countries (primarily South Africa, Mozambique) before delivery. The most common import routes involve sea freight to South African ports (Durban, Port Elizabeth, or Cape Town) followed by road transport through South Africa into Eswatini. UN 38.3 (Recommendations on the Transport of Dangerous Goods, Test and Criteria, Part III, Section 38.3) mandates safety testing (T1–T8) for all lithium cells and batteries transported by any mode — air (IATA DGR), sea (IMDG Code), or road (ADR/SADC-aligned road regulations). A UN 38.3 Test Summary is mandatory for all lithium battery shipments and has been required since 1 January 2020. IEC 62281:2019 (Safety of primary and secondary lithium cells and batteries during transport) provides complementary transport packaging and condition standards.
