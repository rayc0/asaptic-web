Short answer

Canada-bound textiles and apparel are not CE-marked and do not use an EU-style harmonised-standard route. The main gaps for Chinese exporters are: (1) mandatory fibre-content and dealer-identity labelling under the Textile Labelling Act and Textile Labelling and Advertising Regulations, with required information generally in English and French and a dealer name/address or CA identification number; (2) mandatory consumer product safety duties under the Canada Consumer Product Safety Act, including textile flammability rules under SOR/2016-194; (3) children's sleepwear must meet SOR/2016-169 flame resistance and labelling rules; (4) children's products may trigger phthalates and lead restrictions under CCPSA regulations, especially plasticized components, prints, coatings, trims, and accessible parts.

Children's Sleepwear — Canada Consumer Product Safety Act / SOR/2016-169 China's GB 31701-2015 is mandatory for infant and children's textile products and includes chemical, mechanical, and labelling controls. It does not replace Canada's children's sleepwear flame-resistance regulation. Chinese category labels such as infant textile Class A, direct-skin Class B, or non-direct-skin Class C do not determine whether a product is Canadian children's sleepwear. GB 18401-2010 — National General Safety Technical Code for Textile Products (mandatory)		 Children's sleepwear is subject to the Canada Consumer Product Safety Act and the Children's Sleepwear Regulations, SOR/2016-169. The regulation sets mandatory flame-resistance specifications, test procedures, toxicity considerations for flame-retardant treatment, and labelling requirements for covered children's sleepwear. The rule is product-specific and stricter than general apparel screening because sleepwear presents foreseeable ignition and burn risks. Product classification is critical: garments marketed or reasonably likely to be used as children's sleepwear should be assessed under SOR/2016-169 even if sold as loungewear or sets.Canada Consumer Product Safety Act, S.C. 2010, c. 21

Fibre Content and Dealer Identity Labelling — Textile Labelling Act and Regulations China's textile labelling commonly relies on GB/T 29862-2013 for fibre content and GB 5296.4-2012 for instructions for use of textiles and apparel. GB/T 29862 is a recommended national standard but is widely used in supply-chain and platform compliance. GB 5296.4 is mandatory for domestic consumer textile information, including product identification, manufacturer information, size, care, and implementation standard. Chinese labels are normally in Chinese and do not provide Canada's required English/French fibre expressions or Canadian dealer identity.GB/T 29862-2013 — Textiles — Labelling requirements for fibre content (recommended standard, widely referenced)
GB 5296.4-2012 — Instructions for use of products of consumer concern — Part 4: Textiles and apparel (mandatory)		 Canada's Textile Labelling Act and Textile Labelling and Advertising Regulations are the primary mandatory rules for consumer textile articles. Key requirements include accurate fibre content disclosure by generic fibre name and percentage, use of prescribed fibre names and tolerances, and a dealer identity. The dealer identity may be the dealer's name and postal address or, where available, a Canadian CA identification number. Required fibre-content information and prescribed expressions must generally appear in both English and French for the Canadian retail market. Labels must not be false or misleading, and advertising claims must align with the labelled fibre content.Textile Labelling Act, R.S.C., 1985, c. T-10
Textile Labelling and Advertising Regulations, C.R.C., c. 1551
Competition Bureau CA Identification Number program		 The main Canada entry gap is not fibre testing alone but Canadian label execution. Chinese fibre-content reports must be mapped to Canadian generic fibre names and bilingual English/French label wording. A China manufacturer name by itself usually does not satisfy the Canadian dealer identity expectation for retail sale; a Canadian dealer name/address or CA number is normally needed. Claims such as bamboo, wool, down, recycled content, or performance finishes should be checked against Canadian textile advertising rules. Country-of-origin marking may also be required by customs or other programs, but it does not replace Textile Labelling Act fibre and dealer requirements.[INFORMATIONAL] Fibre content and dealer identity labelling are mandatory for covered textiles in Canada. China-only labels and GB-only fibre names are not enough. Prepare English/French fibre composition wording, use Canadian-recognized generic fibre names, and add the Canadian dealer name/address or CA number before retail placement. Lead in Children's Apparel Components and Surface Coatings — CCPSA Regulations China's GB 31701-2015 and GB 18401-2010 focus on textile safety and may not fully cover lead in metal trims, painted components, decorative attachments, or surface coatings as Canadian CCPSA regulations do. Some Chinese product-specific standards or buyer protocols may include heavy metals, but standard fabric reports often omit hardware and coating lead. Exporters need a complete bill of materials and component test plan.GB 31701-2015 — Safety Technical Code for Textile Products for Infants and Young Children (mandatory)
GB 18401-2010 — National General Safety Technical Code for Textile Products (mandatory)		 Under the Canada Consumer Product Safety Act, lead can be regulated through the Consumer Products Containing Lead Regulations, SOR/2018-83, and the Surface Coating Materials Regulations, SOR/2016-193. For apparel, risk areas include painted or coated snaps, buttons, zipper pulls, metal charms, printed or coated decorations, children's jewellery-like attachments, costume parts, and accessible coatings on accessories. Covered products and accessible parts must meet the applicable lead limits; surface coating materials and products with applied coatings are separately controlled.Canada Consumer Product Safety Act, S.C. 2010, c. 21
Consumer Products Containing Lead Regulations, SOR/2018-83
Surface Coating Materials Regulations, SOR/2016-193		 The main gap is that apparel compliance files often test fabric but not accessible trims and coatings. Children's apparel with metal, painted, or coated parts should be treated as a component compliance project: identify every accessible part, determine whether it falls under lead or surface-coating regulations, and test high-risk materials. Avoid assuming that a heavy-metal result for dyed fabric covers snaps, zipper sliders, badges, screen prints, coatings, or costume accessories.[INFORMATIONAL] For children's apparel, lead compliance must cover accessible trims, hardware, prints, coatings, and decorative attachments, not only fabric. Use CCPSA lead and surface-coating regulations to define the component test plan before shipment to Canada. Justice Laws Website / Government of Canada2026-06-12 · unverified
Phthalates in Children's Textile Products — Canada Consumer Product Safety Act / SOR/2016-188 China's GB 31701-2015 and GB 18401-2010 address many textile safety topics but do not provide a complete substitute for Canada's phthalates restrictions in children's products and child-care articles. Chinese apparel testing packages may omit phthalates unless plastic accessories or coatings are specifically identified. Export bills of materials often describe trims generically, so PVC or other plasticized materials may not be visible from the fabric test report.GB 31701-2015 — Safety Technical Code for Textile Products for Infants and Young Children (mandatory)
GB 18401-2010 — National General Safety Technical Code for Textile Products (mandatory)		 The Phthalates Regulations, SOR/2016-188, made under the Canada Consumer Product Safety Act, restrict specified phthalates in covered children's products and child-care articles. For textiles and apparel, the risk is usually in plasticized components rather than the base fabric: PVC prints, plastisol graphics, rubbery appliques, coated labels, zipper pulls, charms, patches, rainwear coatings, bibs, costume accessories, and soft plastic parts that may be mouthed. Exporters should screen all accessible plasticized components in children's apparel and related accessories.Canada Consumer Product Safety Act, S.C. 2010, c. 21
Phthalates Regulations, SOR/2016-188		 The practical gap is component-level testing. A garment may pass Chinese textile chemical tests while a plastic zipper pull, heat-transfer print, badge, or coated accessory contains restricted phthalates. Children's products should be reviewed by age grading, foreseeable mouthing, and accessibility of soft plastic parts. Supplier declarations should be supported by bill-of-material review and targeted testing of plasticized components, especially when using PVC, plastisol, soft rubber, or unknown recycled plastics.[INFORMATIONAL] For children's apparel and accessories, phthalates compliance is a component-level CCPSA issue. Base-fabric GB reports are not enough where PVC, plastisol, soft plastic, rubbery, coated, or mouthing-accessible components are present. General Textile Flammability — Canada Consumer Product Safety Act / SOR/2016-194 China's domestic textile safety framework focuses on GB 18401-2010 for basic chemical and physical safety and GB 31701-2015 for infant and children's textile products. Product-specific Chinese standards may address flame resistance for certain categories, but general apparel complying with GB 18401 is not automatically tested to Canada's textile flammability regulation. Chinese supplier inspection reports may use different conditioning, specimen preparation, fabric-surface, or burn-rate criteria.GB 18401-2010 — National General Safety Technical Code for Textile Products (mandatory)
GB 31701-2015 — Safety Technical Code for Textile Products for Infants and Young Children (mandatory, children's items)		 The Canada Consumer Product Safety Act is the binding consumer product safety law, and the Textile Flammability Regulations, SOR/2016-194, set mandatory flammability specifications for textile products. Covered products must not present prohibited rapid-flame-spread hazards under the prescribed test method and specifications. The rules apply separately from textile fibre labelling and can affect apparel fabrics, costumes, bedding, household textiles, and other textile consumer products unless a product-specific regulation applies. Compliance evidence should be based on the Canadian regulation and its incorporated test approach, not merely on a supplier declaration.Canada Consumer Product Safety Act, S.C. 2010, c. 21
Textile Flammability Regulations, SOR/2016-194		 A common gap is assuming that general China textile safety testing covers Canadian burn-performance rules. Canada requires evaluation against SOR/2016-194 or an applicable product-specific Canadian regulation. Raised-fibre fabrics, costumes, loose-fitting garments, brushed fleece, chenille, pile fabrics, and lightweight synthetics should be screened early. Any flame-retardant treatment also needs chemical and durability review, because passing flammability by treatment does not remove obligations under CCPSA or other chemical restrictions.[INFORMATIONAL] Canadian textile flammability compliance is mandatory and separate from fibre labelling. Do not rely on GB 18401 or a generic supplier statement for Canada-bound goods; obtain product-specific evidence against SOR/2016-194 or the applicable Canadian product regulation.
