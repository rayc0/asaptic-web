Short answer

Niger does not have a confirmed standalone mandatory BESS product safety regulation with an established national conformity assessment route equivalent to a CE mark or SABER certificate. BESS projects in Niger are almost exclusively donor-financed (World Bank, AfDB, EU) solar-plus-storage mini-grid or grid-stability installations and therefore operate under the procurement standards set by lenders and project owners, which typically require IEC 62619 cell and module safety evidence, IEC 62933 system-level compliance, and UN 38.3 transport documentation. ARSE (Autorité de Régulation du Secteur de l'Électricité) is the electricity sector regulator and NIGELEC is the national utility — both must approve grid-connected BESS. The practical compliance gates for Chinese BESS exported to Niger are: (1) IEC 62619 cell and module safety evidence as the project-acceptance baseline required by donor lenders and project owners; (2) IEC 62933 system-level technical requirements where referenced in NIGELEC connection conditions or project-owner specifications; (3) ARSE licensing and NIGELEC grid-connection technical approval; (4) donor/lender environmental and social standards (World Bank Group Environmental and Social Framework) for battery chemistry and end-of-life management; (5) local fire and building authority installation approval; (6) UN 38.3 transport documentation and landlocked transit logistics (Benin/Togo/Ghana coastal corridors) requiring multi-country dangerous-goods clearance; and (7) extreme environment derating — Niger's Saharan/Sahelian climate (ambient ≥45°C, severe dust/sand) requires evidence of high-temperature cycle life and IP-rated enclosures adequate for operating conditions. Chinese GB/T 36558-2023, GB 44240-2024, and GB/T 36276-2023 represent the manufacturing baseline but are not accepted as substitutes for IEC 62619 evidence in donor-funded project review.

Since January 1, 2020, a UN 38.3 Test Summary is mandatory documentation under international transport regulations (IATA DGR, IMDG Code, ADR). Niger is a landlocked country with no direct sea access. All ocean-freight BESS shipments from China must transit through one of three main coastal corridor ports before overland transport to Niger: (a) Cotonou Port, Benin — the primary corridor for Niger imports, using the road/rail corridor via Parakou; (b) Lomé Port, Togo — an increasingly used alternative corridor; (c) Tema Port, Ghana — used for some Niger-bound shipments. Each transit country (Benin, Togo, Ghana) applies its own domestic dangerous-goods regulations and may require: transit permits for lithium battery shipments, compliance with ECOWAS/bilateral transport agreements, and separate customs and transit bond documentation. Air freight (Diori Hamani International Airport, Niamey) is also subject to IATA DGR requirements for lithium batteries. The entire logistics chain — ocean freight (IMDG Code), port handling in Benin/Togo/Ghana, road transit through transit countries, and delivery to Niger — must comply with UN 38.3 requirements.
