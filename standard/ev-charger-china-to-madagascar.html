Short answer

For China-made EV chargers entering Madagascar, the confirmed practical gaps are: IEC-based safety evidence required under BNM-adopted IEC standards (IEC 61851-1 / IEC 61851-23) and coordinated with ORE; connector hardware redesign from GB/T 20234 to IEC 62196 Type 2 (AC) / CCS2 (DC), which is physically incompatible with GB/T connectors; ORE regulatory approval and JIRAMA grid-connection coordination for any installation on Madagascar's national grid; EMC conformity to IEC 61000 series; and OCPP back-office protocol for any networked deployment. Madagascar operates on 220/380 V, 50 Hz — the same nominal voltage as China's domestic baseline — but grid reliability is severely constrained by JIRAMA's infrastructure, and voltage fluctuations, outages, and low rural electrification rates are significant deployment risks. Critically, Madagascar's EV market is extremely nascent: domestic EV penetration is near zero, and charging infrastructure deployment faces fundamental constraints from low electrification rates, fuel-import dependency, and limited grid capacity. Exporters must assess not only technical compliance but whether viable deployment conditions exist for the specific project and location. BNM conformity assessment and ORE/JIRAMA coordination are the regulatory gates before any installation.

Connector Interoperability — GB/T 20234 vs IEC 62196 Type 2 / CCS2 China AC chargers use GB/T 20234.2 couplers and DC fast chargers use GB/T 20234.3 couplers.
